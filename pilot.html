Engines & Accessories
Cheat Sheet · Engines and Accessories section
Left engine rotates CW, right CCW — viewed from the pilot seat. Because of this counter-rotation, there is no critical engine on the Chieftain.
  • L (CW) / R (CCW) — viewed from pilot seat. No critical engine.
  • Geared starter with starter ring gear
  • Single driven dual magnetos
  • 28V / 70A alternator per engine
  • Turbochargers — density controller maintains full rated power to critical altitude
  • Differential pressure controller — auto-adjusts MP during climb/descent
  • Hydraulic pumps, oil filters, oil coolers, pneumatic pressure pumps
🌀
Propellers
Cheat Sheet · Propellers section
3-blade constant-speed, controllable pitch, full feathering props. Feathering requires above 1,000 RPM and depends on engine oil pressure.
⛔
Critical — Engine Oil LossThe propeller will move toward feather as soon as engine oil is lost. Feather it above 1,000 RPM.
⚠️
RPM OverspeedDo NOT shut down the engine if the prop cannot be feathered during an overspeed. A windmilling prop in low pitch creates extreme drag.
🛫
Flaps — Duke & Calco Systems
Cheat Sheet · Flaps section
Two possible flap systems: Duke or Calco. Both allow 0–40°. Confirm which your aircraft has. Never use autopilot with flaps beyond 15°.
📌
Duke SystemIf flaps don't extend together within ~1 second (~9° asymmetry), time delay relay shuts off motor. Reset only by cycling the master switch — do not reset until landed.
📌
Calco SystemShuts down if R & L rheostats differ by more than 5°. Flap Fault Test switch tests system integrity. Light ON = OK · No light = fault.
⛔
Asymmetric Flap FlightMax asymmetry (0° vs 25°) requires ~85% aileron deflection to hold wings level. Final approach: 100 kts, 500–800 fpm descent.
🛞
Landing Gear
Cheat Sheet · Landing Gear section
Hydraulically actuated gear. Normal cycle: 6 seconds. Green = down/locked. Red = in transition. No light = up/locked. Unsafe horn sounds when MP < 12 and gear isn't down and locked.
Green = Down & Locked Red = In Transition / Door Open No Light = Up & Locked
⛔
TowingNever tow without removing the tow pin. Total turning arc is 40° (20° each direction).
🔴
Brake System
Cheat Sheet · Brake System section
Hydraulically actuated by master cylinders on the left rudder pedals. Brake hydraulic reservoir is independent from the main hydraulic system.
⛔
Parking BrakeAlways press the pedals first, THEN set the parking brake. Pulling the handle before pressing pedals may result in no braking.

De-Icing & Anti-Ice Systems
Cheat Sheet · Wings and empennage deicing section
Boots on wings and tail, dual heated pitot tubes, electrically heated windshield (captain's side only), and electric prop de-ice strips. Do not use boots below −20°C.
  • Heated pitot tubes — dual
  • Heated windshield — captain's side only
  • De-ice boots — wings and stabilizer leading edges
  • Electric prop de-ice strips — 30 sec per segment, timer-controlled
⚠️
Boot LimitationsMax speed for boot inflation: 213 kts CAS. Do not use below −20°C — rubber can crack.
📌
Prop De-Ice SequenceOutboard RE → Inboard RE → Outboard LE → Inboard LE. Ammeter: 8–12A when ON.

Heating & Ventilation
Cheat Sheet · Heat, Ventilating and Defrosting section
Janitrol combustion heater in the right nose. Draws fuel from R/H inboard tank at ~2 gal/hour. All Skycare Navajos have at least the L/H engine muff system.
⚠️

Flight Preparation & Loading
Cheat Sheet · Flight Preparation & Loading sections
Check the last 5 pages of the logbook for snags. Check all 4 nose bowl screws and 6 cowling fasteners (3 per side). A loose fastener can cause in-flight cowl separation.
  • Check logbook — read last 5 flight pages
  • Walk-around and run-up — focus on recently fixed items
  • Check all 4 nose bowl screws present and secure
  • Check 6 cowling fasteners — red-on-red stripe alignment and locking pin extended

Key Definitions
CAP GEN · Definitions pages 9–14
These are the core IFR terms you need to know cold. DA vs MDA is the most important distinction — DA is used on precision approaches (you initiate missed approach AT the DA), MDA is used on non-precision (you level off at MDA and look for visual reference before descending further).
TermPlain English
DA (Decision Altitude)The altitude on a precision or APV approach at which you MUST initiate missed approach if required visual reference is not established. Referenced to MSL.
DH (Decision Height)The height of the DA above the touchdown zone elevation (TDZE). Referenced to the runway.
MDA (Min Descent Altitude)The lowest altitude on a non-precision approach you may descend to until you have the required visual reference. You level off here — you do NOT continue descending below MDA until the runway is in sight.
HAT (Height Above Touchdown)The height of the DA or MDA above the TDZE. Charted for some straight-in minima.
HAA (Height Above Aerodrome)The height of the MDA above aerodrome elevation. Charted for all circling minima.
HATh (Height Above Threshold)Height of DA or MDA above the runway threshold elevation.
MSA (Min Sector Altitude)Lowest altitude providing 1,000 ft clearance above all obstacles within a 25 NM sector of a navaid or waypoint. Emergency use only — not for normal ops.
MAP (Missed Approach Point)The point at which a missed approach must be initiated if visual reference has not been established.
FAF (Final Approach Fix)The fix where the final approach segment begins. Where you descend to MDA or follow the glidepath to DA.
RVR (Runway Visual Range)Measured in hundreds of feet. The distance a pilot can see down the runway centreline. More precise than ground visibility — used for approach bans.
TORATake-Off Run Available — length of runway for the ground roll.
TODATake-Off Distance Available — TORA + any clearway beyond.
ASDAAccelerate-Stop Distance Available — TORA + stopway. Used for rejected takeoff planning.
LDALanding Distance Available — runway length declared suitable for landing.
TCH (Threshold Crossing Height)Height of the glide path above the runway threshold. Typically 50 ft for most ILS approaches.
TDZE (Touchdown Zone Elevation)The highest elevation in the first 3,000 ft (or first ⅓) of the runway from the threshold.
ATFAerodrome Traffic Frequency Area — VHF frequency for common reporting. Not ATC.
MFMandatory Frequency Area — legally required to monitor and report on this frequency (CARs 602.97–602.103).
Step-Down FixA fix in a non-precision approach that allows additional descent once passed, by confirming obstacle clearance to that point.
Dead Reckoning SegmentA segment flown by heading, time, and speed when no fix is available. Heading shown intercepts the inbound track prior to the IF.

PA31 is a Non-Jet AircraftThe CAP GEN explicitly lists PA31 as a "non jet aircraft" (turbo prop / piston). This affects which SID/STAR procedures you can fly — some are jet-only or non-jet-only.

Operating Minima — General
CAP GEN · pages 16–18
CAR 602 governs: takeoffs by visibility only, approaches by RVR only, landings by published DH/MDA only. The aerodrome operating visibility determines what counts — the hierarchy differs at controlled vs uncontrolled aerodromes.
📌
At Controlled Aerodromes (active ATC tower) — Arrival & Departure Hierarchy 1. RVR for the runway in use
2. Ground visibility (METAR)
3. Tower visibility
4. Pilot visibility
Note: Tower visibility is advisory only when ground visibility is reported.
📌
At Uncontrolled Aerodromes — Arrival Hierarchy 1. RVR for runway in use
2. Ground visibility (METAR)
3. Pilot visibility
📌
At Uncontrolled Aerodromes — Departure Lowest of: Ground visibility (METAR) · Any reported RVR · Pilot visibility
⚠️
Low Visibility Procedures (LVP)Apply when RVR is below 2,600 ft (imminent). ATIS will broadcast: "LOW VISIBILITY PROCEDURES IN EFFECT" or "REDUCED VISIBILITY PROCEDURES IN EFFECT." Arrivals and departures below RVR 600 are not authorized.

Take-off Minima / Departure Procedures
CAP GEN · pages 19–21
IFR takeoffs are prohibited below the published minimum visibility. The published departure procedure assumes you cross the runway end at 35 ft, climb runway heading to 400 ft AAE before turning, and maintain at least 200 ft/NM climb gradient to minimum enroute altitude.
📌
Take-off Visibility — Order of Precedence 1. Reported RVR for the runway (unless fluctuating or below minimum due to local phenomenon)
2. Reported ground visibility (if RVR unavailable or fluctuating)
3. Pilot-observed visibility (when neither above is available)
📌
Departure Premises (assumed for obstacle clearance) • Cross at least 35 ft above the departure end of the runway
• Climb runway heading to 400 ft AAE before turning
• Maintain at least 200 ft/NM climb gradient throughout climb to minimum enroute altitude
⛔
NOT ASSESSED RunwaysIf a runway is marked "NOT ASSESSED," IFR departures have not been evaluated for obstacles. YOU are responsible for your own obstacle clearance. Minimum visibility: ½ SM (¼ SM for rotorcraft).

Approach Ban
CAP GEN · pages 23–27 · CARs 602.129, 602.130, 700.10, 700.11
The approach ban prohibits continuing past the FAF inbound (or equivalent) if RVR/visibility is below specified minima. Key principle: if you've already passed the FAF when a below-minima report is received, you may continue. RVR takes precedence over all other visibility reports.
📌
General Aviation — Non-Precision / APV / CAT IAn approach is authorized whenever ANY of these apply:
• Lowest reported RVR at or above minima
• RVR is fluctuating above and below minimum
• Ground visibility reported at least ¼ SM
• RVR unavailable or not reported
• ATS notified it's a training flight with planned missed approach

Landing Minima
CAP GEN · pages 29–30 · CARs 602
Landings are governed by published DH/MDA. Published advisory visibilities are not limiting — they indicate what visibility should give you the required visual reference. The approach ban is what's legally limiting, not the landing visibility.
⛔
Critical RuleYou must not continue descent below DH or below MDA until the required visual reference is established AND maintained. If you lose visual reference below MDA, you must initiate a missed approach. Missed approaches beyond the MAP may not be assured obstacle clearance.
📌
Required Visual References — at least ONE of: Runway or markings · Runway threshold or markings · Touchdown zone or markings · Approach lights · Approach slope indicator · Runway identification lights (RILS) · Threshold and runway end lights · Touchdown zone lights (TDZL) · Parallel runway edge lights · Runway centreline lights
📌
Altimeter SettingMust be set before commencing an instrument approach. Altimeter settings are considered current up to 90 minutes from time of observation.
Caution: If older than 60 minutes or pressure falling rapidly — add correction: 0.01" Hg = 10 ft correction.

Cold Temperature Altitude Corrections
CAP GEN · pages 36–37
Pressure altimeters are calibrated for ISA conditions. In cold temperatures, true altitude is LOWER than indicated altitude — you are actually lower than your altimeter shows. You must add a correction to published minimum IFR altitudes when temperature is 0°C or colder (or +10°C when MDA/DA is 1,000 ft HAA or higher).
⛔
Critical RuleTrue altitude is lower than indicated when temperature is below ISA. In cold weather, you need MORE indicated altitude to achieve the same true altitude obstacle clearance. Add the correction to published minimums.
📌
When to Apply • Below 0°C surface temperature — apply for all published minimum IFR altitudes

Noise Abatement Procedures (NADP)
CAP GEN · pages 34–35
Two NADP profiles exist. NADP1 reduces noise close to the departure end. NADP2 reduces noise in areas more distant from the runway. Both must still meet required minimum climb gradients specified in the SID.
📌
NADP 1 — Close-in Noise Reduction • Climb to at least 800 ft AAE at takeoff power, T/O flaps, V2 + 10 kt
• At or above 800 ft: reduce power/thrust, maintain V2 + 10–20 kt, keep T/O flaps
• At 3,000 ft AAE: positive rate, accelerate to enroute climb speed, retract flaps on schedule
Notify ATC clearance delivery if using NADP1
📌
NADP 2 — Distant Noise Reduction • Climb to at least 800 ft AAE at takeoff power, T/O flaps, V2 + 10 kt
• At or above 800 ft: positive rate, accelerate toward VZF, retract flaps on schedule, reduce power within acceleration segment
• Continue climb to 3,000 ft at ≥ VZF
• At 3,000 ft: transition to normal enroute climb speed

Feathering RequirementsFeather above 1,000 RPM. If the engine has lost oil pressure, the prop will migrate toward feather automatically. A windmilling prop (unfeathered, low pitch) creates massive drag — equivalent to deploying a speed brake. Never leave a failed engine windmilling if feathering is possible.
