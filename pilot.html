Fuel System
Cheat Sheet · Fuel Limit & Fuel System sections
The Chieftain runs on AvGas. It has main inboard tanks, outboard tanks, and optional nacelle (Nayak) tanks. Always depart on mains. Keep fuel balanced within 200 lbs. The nacelle tanks take ~55 minutes to transfer fully. Rule of thumb: 1 L/min/engine at cruise.

Fuel PathFuel cells → Selector valve → Fuel filter → Low fuel boost pump → Emergency fuel pump → Firewall shutoff valve → Engine-driven fuel pump → Injectors → Cylinders.
⛔
WarningIf the L or R low boost warning light illuminates with fuel showing in the inboard tank → flapper door malfunction → immediately switch to outboard tank or crossfeed to avoid fuel flow interruption.
⚠️
Fuel Management Rules• Mains for T/O and landing always
• Fuel must be balanced within 200 lbs prior to departure
• Do not transfer nacelle fuel until mains are at 50% or less
• Do not use outboard tanks during climbs or descents
• No prolonged slips/skids (30+ sec) when on outboard or with inboards below ¼
• Crossfeed is NOT for normal ops
📌
NoteCannot dispatch with inop engine-driven fuel pump or inop emergency fuel pump. Low boost pump warning illuminates if fuel boost pressure drops below 3 PSI.
💡
Rule of Thumb1 L/min/engine at cruise. For more accuracy, do the math from your fuel flow gauge.

Oil System
Cheat Sheet · Oil Capacity section
Each engine holds 12 quarts max. You need at least 10 quarts per engine to dispatch. Oil does three things: lubricates, cools, and cleans the engine. Watch the dipstick carefully — the same dipstick is used for both engines with different markings on each side.

Powerplant & Power Settings
Cheat Sheet · Power Plant Limitation & Power Setting sections
Both engines are turbocharged, counter-rotating, fuel-injected Lycomings. No critical engine on the Chieftain. The density controller maintains full rated power to critical altitude (49 inHg / 350 HP). Make all throttle and prop changes slowly and smoothly.

Cruise Power TechniqueSet ~26–27 MP at 2,200 RPM → power will settle around 28 MP → lean to Rich of Peak (~−50° EGT) → adjust as needed. Always synchronize props — unsynchronized props make the cabin very noisy.

BLR Modification
Cheat Sheet · BLR Kit section
BLR (Boundary Layer Research) is a performance modification that improves stall characteristics and increases allowable weights. The vortex generators must be checked on every preflight — if fewer than 84 are in place, you fall back to original AFM limits.
4 Engine Nacelle Strakes 88 Vortex Generators MLG Modification
📌
Benefits• Reduced stall speed (Vs) and minimum control speed (Vmca)
• Increased T/O and landing weight limits
⛔
Vortex Generator CheckIf fewer than 84 of the 88 vortex generators are in place, the aircraft must be operated under original AFM limitations — not the improved BLR limits.

Engines & Accessories
Cheat Sheet · Engines and Accessories section
Left engine rotates clockwise (CW), right engine counter-clockwise (CCW) — viewed from the pilot seat. Because of this counter-rotation, there is no critical engine on the Chieftain. Each engine drives multiple systems including alternators, hydraulic pumps, and pneumatic pumps.
  • L (CW) / R (CCW) — viewed from pilot seat. No critical engine.
  • Geared starter with starter ring gear
  • Single driven dual magnetos (some aircraft: 2 magnetos per side)
  • 28V / 70A alternator per engine
  • Shielded ignition system
  • Turbochargers — density controller maintains full rated power to critical altitude
  • Differential pressure controller — automatically adjusts MP during climb/descent, eliminating the need for pilot throttle adjustment
  • Hydraulic pumps, oil filters, oil coolers, pneumatic pressure pumps
🌀
Propellers
Cheat Sheet · Propellers section
3-blade constant-speed, controllable pitch, full feathering props. High pitch (feathered) is driven by nitrogen/air pressure + springs. Low pitch (higher RPM) is driven by governor-regulated oil pressure. Feathering requires above 1,000 RPM and depends on engine oil pressure.
⛔
Critical — Engine Oil LossThe propeller will move toward feather as soon as engine oil is lost. Feather it above 1,000 RPM.
⚠️
RPM OverspeedDo NOT shut down the engine if the prop cannot be feathered during an overspeed. A windmilling prop in low pitch creates extreme drag. Do not secure the engine if the propeller cannot be feathered.
📌
RuleMake all throttle and prop changes slowly and smoothly.

Flaps — Duke & Calco Systems
Cheat Sheet · Flaps section
The Chieftain can have one of two flap systems: Duke or Calco. Both have asymmetry protection but work differently. Confirm which system your specific aircraft has. Both allow 0–40° deflection. Never use the autopilot with flaps beyond 15°.
Duke: 0–40° with time delay relay Calco: 0–40° in 2° increments with 5° asymmetry limit
📌
Duke SystemIf flaps don't extend together within ~1 second (~9° of asymmetry), the time delay relay shuts off the motor and the selector becomes inoperative. Only way to reset: cycle the master switch. Do not reset until landed and issue resolved.
📌
Calco SystemElectronically monitored. Will shut down if R & L rheostats differ by more than 5°. Flap Fault Test switch tests the system integrity (not just the bulb). Light ON = system OK · No light = fault.
⛔
Asymmetric Flap Flight (Both Systems)Max asymmetry (0° vs 25°) requires ~85% aileron deflection to hold wings level. Minimize bank angles. Plan a higher, wider pattern. Final approach: 100 kts, 500–800 fpm descent. Do not use autopilot with flaps >15°.

Landing Gear
Cheat Sheet · Landing Gear section
Hydraulically actuated gear with air-oil oleo struts. Normal cycle takes 6 seconds. Green lights = down and locked. Red light = in transition or inboard door open. No light at all = up and locked. The unsafe horn sounds when MP is below 12 and gear isn't down and locked.
Green = Down & Locked (one per gear) Red = In Transition / Door Open No Light = Up & Locked
⚠️
Gear Horn ConditionsSounds when: MP < 12 inHg AND gear not down/locked, OR gear selector is up/neutral with aircraft on ground and master ON.
⛔
TowingNever tow without removing the tow pin. Avoid sharp turns — causes excessive tire wear. Total turning arc is 40° (20° each direction).
💡
Practical TipWhen the gear reaches its desired position, hydraulic force pushes the selector back to center/neutral automatically. Selector at neutral = cycle complete. A missing light is likely a burnt bulb — but always use gear position lights as primary indication.

Brake System
Cheat Sheet · Brake System section
Hydraulically actuated by individual master cylinders on the left rudder pedals (optional on right). The brake system has its own separate hydraulic reservoir — independent from the main hydraulic system.
⛔
Parking BrakeBraking may NOT occur if the parking brake handle is pulled before applying the brake pedals. Always press the pedals first, then set the parking brake.
💧
Hydraulic System
Cheat Sheet · Hydraulic System section
Two engine-driven hydraulic pumps supply pressure for gear operation. A central power pack controls the system. There's also an emergency hand pump for use in case of electrical failure — it opens the gear doors and allows gear extension.
  • 2 engine-driven hydraulic pumps — primary gear operation source
  • Power pack — central control unit
  • Emergency hand pump — use if electrical failure; opens doors and lowers gear
  • Actuating cylinders, filters

Pneumatic System
Cheat Sheet · Pneumatic System section
Two engine-driven pneumatic pumps supply suction for the gyro instruments (attitude and heading indicator) and power the wing/tail de-icing boots. Normal suction is 5.5 ± 0.4 inHg at 2,200 RPM. The turn & slip indicator is electric — serves as standby if pneumatic fails.

De-Icing & Anti-Ice Systems
Cheat Sheet · Wings and empennage deicing section
The Chieftain has boots on wings and tail, heated pitot tubes (dual), an electrically heated windshield (captain's side only), and electric prop de-ice strips. Boots inflate for 6 or 12 seconds per cycle. The blue indicator light confirms inflation at 18 PSI. Do not use boots below −20°C.
  • Heated pitot tubes — dual
  • Heated windshield — captain's side only (electric)
  • De-ice boots — wings and H/V stabilizer leading edges
  • Electric prop de-ice strips — timer-controlled, 30 sec per segment
  • Wing ice light — left wing only
⚠️
Boot LimitationsMax speed for boot inflation: 213 kts CAS. Do not use or test boots below −20°C — rubber can crack and ice won't adhere at that temperature anyway. Suction keeps boots flat (streamlined) when system is OFF.
📌
Prop De-Ice Timer SequenceSegments cycle every 30 sec: Outboard RE → Inboard RE → Outboard LE → Inboard LE. Ammeter should read 8–12A when switch is ON.
⚠️
Ground OperationsDo not run pitot heat for extended periods on the ground. Do not run heated windshield on ground for extended periods unless ice is present (risk of overheating).

Heating & Ventilation
Cheat Sheet · Heat, Ventilating and Defrosting section
Standard config has a Janitrol combustion heater in the right nose section for cabin heat and windshield defrost. It draws fuel from the R/H inboard tank at ~2 gal/hour. Optional configurations include a left-side airborne heater, an aft Janitrol, and exhaust muff heaters. All Skycare Navajos have at least the L/H engine muff system.
⚠️
Heater Air InletThe heater air inlet valve must be fully open before and during heater operation. If partially closed, the heater will overheat and shut down.
⚠️
Shutdown Procedure (Ground Only)Turn to FAN position for 3 minutes to cool the heater before switching to OFF. In flight this is not required.
📌
Stall WarningWarning activates 4–10 kts before actual stall. On some aircraft, the stall detector vane is heated when the Left Pitot heat is ON — but only in flight.

Instruments & Annunciator Panel
Cheat Sheet · Instrument Panel & Annunciator Panel sections
Standard AI and HI are pneumatically driven. The turn & slip indicator is electric (standby backup). The annunciator panel press-to-test requires 3 seconds to illuminate all lights — the low fuel flow warning stays on 10 seconds longer than the others.

Flight Preparation & Loading
Cheat Sheet · Flight Preparation & Loading sections
Check the last 5 pages of the logbook for snags. If something was just fixed — focus on that item during your run-up. Load strategically based on passenger/cargo weight distribution. Cowl fasteners are critical — a missing or loose fastener can cause cowl separation in flight.
  • Check logbook for snags, deferred items, time remaining — read last 5 flight pages
  • Walk-around and run-up — focus on recently fixed items
  • Take water, commissary, and lunches
  • Check all 4 nose bowl screws present and secure
  • Check 6 side cowling fasteners (3 per side) — look for red-on-red stripe alignment and locking pin extended
⛔
Cowl Fasteners — CriticalFailure to ensure nose bowl and cowl fasteners are present and secure may result in in-flight cowl separation and loss of aircraft control.
💡
Loading StrategyLight load → weight aft · Medium → center · Full load → concentrate weight forward · Small dense items in nose and wing lockers.
